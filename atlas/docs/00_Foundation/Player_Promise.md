# Player Promise

This document defines what _The Last Sword Protocol_ promises to the player.

It is not marketing copy.

It is a design contract.

---

## 1. Exploration Is Never Wasted

If the player notices something strange and investigates, the game should usually reward them.

The reward does not always need to be gold or equipment.

It may be:

- a memory fragment,
- a joke,
- a hidden item,
- a shortcut,
- a strange object,
- a lore clue,
- a recurring NPC moment,
- a beautiful view,
- an optional monster,
- a clue that matters much later.

The world should teach the player that curiosity is worthwhile.

---

## 2. The Main Story Must Be Finishable Without A Guide

Optional secrets can be obscure.

The main story cannot be.

The player should always have enough information to continue if they talk to NPCs, observe the world, and follow the story.

Classic Dragon Quest-style clues are encouraged. Arbitrary progress blockers are not.

---

## 3. Optional Secrets Should Reward Observation

Hidden content should feel earned.

Examples:

- An NPC mentions walking four steps south of a landmark.
- A lighthouse points toward a hidden reef after it is repaired.
- A strange tree appears in every old map but not on the current world map.
- A recurring traveler drops hints about ruins they failed to understand.

Secrets should make the player feel clever, not cheated.

---

## 4. NPCs Matter

NPCs should not exist only to deliver instructions.

They should reveal culture, humor, fear, misunderstanding, hope, and the consequences of the player’s actions.

When the player returns to a town after solving a crisis, NPCs should change.

A world that remembers the player feels alive.

---

## 5. Mystery Should Feel Fair

The player will not know everything early.

That is intentional.

However, the clues must be real.

When the truth is revealed, the player should be able to look back and think:

> The signs were there.

The game should never rely on a twist that contradicts earlier evidence.

---

## 6. Combat Should Be Familiar, Clear, And Charming

Combat should feel like a classic turn-based JRPG.

Enemy families should be readable at a glance.

Recolored and upgraded variants are encouraged.

The player should learn enemy behavior through repeated encounters, not complicated tutorials.

---

## 7. The Ending Should Leave Hope

The ending may be emotional.

It may acknowledge loss.

But it should not leave the player in despair.

The final emotional note should be peace, restoration, and the future.

The world does not need a chosen hero forever.

It needs people willing to live wisely.
