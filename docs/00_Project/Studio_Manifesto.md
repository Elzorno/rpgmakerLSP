# Studio Manifesto

This document defines how *The Last Sword Protocol* thinks.

It is written for humans and AI agents working on the project.

Before adding a map, monster, quest, line of dialogue, item, animation, or plugin, contributors should understand this manifesto.

---

## We Build Worlds, Not Levels

A map should never exist only because the player needs somewhere to fight enemies.

Every place should feel like it existed before Kai arrived and will continue existing after he leaves.

A town needs work, food, family, local habits, rumors, fears, jokes, children, elders, buildings with purpose, and at least one mystery.

A dungeon needs history, not just treasure.

A ruin needs a forgotten function.

---

## We Build Villages, Not Quest Hubs

A Dragon Quest-inspired village is memorable because it has personality.

Ashford should feel warm and lived in.

Coalmouth should feel tired, smoky, and stubborn.

The Athenaeum should feel quiet, brilliant, and dangerous in a way scholars refuse to admit.

New Meridian should feel like civilization almost remembered how to be modern.

Every settlement must answer three questions:

1. Why do people live here?
2. What do they believe about the old world?
3. What is wrong beneath the surface?

---

## We Build Families, Not NPCs

NPCs should not feel like signposts with names.

They should have jobs, relationships, habits, and opinions that sometimes contradict each other.

A child who dreams of adventure in Act I may train with a wooden sword in Act II and stand guard near the endgame.

A grandmother who preserves a warning she does not understand may be one of the most important protectors in history.

Small human continuity matters.

---

## We Build History, Not Lore Dumps

The player should discover history through places, objects, memories, contradictions, jokes, local myths, and environmental details.

Avoid long explanations when the player can infer the truth.

A broken terminal under an altar says more than a scholar explaining that temples were once infrastructure facilities.

---

## We Build Mystery, Not Exposition

Every answer should create at least one new question.

The Sword should not immediately explain the world.

It should authenticate Kai, recover only fragments, and leave the player wondering what those fragments mean.

The true nature of NEMESIS should be hinted at long before it is named.

---

## We Build Hope, Not Despair

The world has suffered, but it has not died.

Children still laugh.

Bread still bakes.

Music still exists.

People still fall in love.

Someone still plants flowers near a broken machine because the flowers make the place less sad.

Hope is not decorative. It is the reason the world is worth saving.

---

## We Build Trust, Not Gimmicks

The hidden cybersecurity layer is not a joke and not a gimmick.

It is the secret logic of the world.

Fantasy language and engineering reality should both be true at the same time.

A magic seal can be emotionally meaningful to villagers while also being an encryption boundary in the hidden design layer.

A blessing can feel sacred while secretly functioning as authentication.

A curse can terrify a village while behaving consistently like compromise or malware.

---

## We Respect Dragon Quest Without Copying It

Dragon Quest is the primary emotional inspiration:

- warm towns,
- clear adventure structure,
- charming monsters,
- exploration rewards,
- secrets,
- simple but satisfying progression,
- humor beside danger,
- ordinary people who matter.

The project must not copy Dragon Quest characters, monsters, places, terms, or copyrighted elements.

We preserve the spirit, not the surface.

---

## The Studio Test

Before accepting any new idea, ask:

1. Does this increase wonder?
2. Does this reward curiosity?
3. Does this reinforce hope?
4. Does this obey the hidden technology layer?
5. Does this feel possible in RPG Maker MZ?
6. Would it still be meaningful without explaining the cybersecurity metaphor?

If the answer to most of these is no, the idea does not belong yet.
